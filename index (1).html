2. Calm guide

The bathroom can be a safe space where you take a breath and recalibrate. It’s absolutely fine to relax here for at least ten minutes – this is your “me time.”

  • Deep breathing: Sit comfortably with your feet hip‑width apart. Let your breath flow as deeply as is comfortable, in through your nose and out through your mouth【769273773202455†L60-L74】. Breathe in gently, counting slowly from 1 to 5, then let it flow out gently, counting from 1 to 5 again【769273773202455†L60-L74】. Continue for at least five minutes.
  • Mindfulness: Spend 5–10 minutes focusing on your breath or bodily sensations, letting go of distractions【306844517947482†L219-L230】. Apps like Headspace or Calm can help【306844517947482†L219-L230】.
  • Exercise: Moderate activity releases endorphins and lowers anxiety – a quick walk, some yoga or stretching in the bathroom can help【306844517947482†L231-L241】.
  • Gratitude and positivity: Write down three things that went well today or that you’re grateful for【306844517947482†L273-L281】.
  • CBT techniques: When anxious thoughts arise, ask yourself whether they are realistic or exaggerated【306844517947482†L252-L261】. Reframe catastrophising thoughts with balanced perspectives【306844517947482†L252-L261】.

Other self‑care tools: keep a small cache of uplifting items in your bathroom. A spare phone charger, a written Wi‑Fi password, comics or inspirational books, and a scented candle can make the space feel like a refuge.

If your anxiety is chronic or overwhelming, consider professional support (see §8).

3. Danger / Emergency / Accident

If you or someone else is in immediate danger:

  • Call 112 for fire or medical emergencies【208495937252610†L70-L75】. Calls are free and operators usually speak English and German【208495937252610†L70-L78】. Stay on the line, state the incident, give your exact location and answer questions until told to hang up【208495937252610†L80-L87】.
  • Call 110 for police emergencies, e.g., assault or threats【208495937252610†L96-L100】.
  • When calling 112, be ready to say where you are, what has happened, how many people are involved and what injuries are present【338337483410053†L228-L239】.
  • Perform first aid while waiting. If someone is unconscious but breathing, put them in the recovery position and check their breathing regularly【338337483410053†L176-L186】. If the patient stops breathing, lay them on their back and start chest compressions【338337483410053†L180-L181】. Use a defibrillator if available【338337483410053†L182-L183】.
  • To stop heavy bleeding, wear gloves, elevate the limb and press a clean cloth against the wound; apply a compression bandage and call 112 immediately【338337483410053†L285-L301】.
  • Remember: you are legally obliged to help within your capabilities【338337483410053†L174-L184】. Even if your first aid isn’t perfect, good‑faith attempts are protected under German law【338337483410053†L184-L186】.

4. Self‑ambulance guide (pain / harm)

This section covers minor injuries and non‑life‑threatening ailments. For anything serious, call 112 or 110 as described above.

  1. Assess: Identify the type of pain (cut, burn, stomach cramp, headache). If you feel faint or have chest pain, dizziness, severe bleeding or difficulty breathing, stop reading and call 112 immediately.
  2. Clean wounds: Rinse cuts or abrasions with clean water, remove debris and apply antiseptic. Cover with a sterile bandage.
  3. Minor burns: Cool under running water for at least ten minutes. Do not apply creams or ice.
  4. Congestion / digestive issues: Stay hydrated, massage your abdomen gently and consider natural remedies (prune juice, warm tea). If constipation lasts more than a few days or is accompanied by severe pain, see a doctor.
  5. Headache or cramps: Take time to breathe and stretch (§2). Over‑the‑counter pain relief may help, but check dosages and contraindications.
  6. Aftercare: Monitor symptoms. If pain worsens or you feel anxious, contact a healthcare professional or reach out to the helplines in §8.

5. Self‑defence & ethics

German law (Section 32 of the Strafgesetzbuch) permits you to defend yourself against an unlawful attack by any means necessary【170788344385436†L160-L176】. You do not have to retreat or wait for police【170788344385436†L168-L171】. However, there are important limitations:

  • Force must end the current attack; once the threat ends, your right to self‑defence ends【170788344385436†L181-L203】.
  • You cannot harm people who clearly cannot understand the situation (e.g., children stealing apples)【170788344385436†L186-L188】.
  • Avoid “grossly disproportionate” responses; the chosen action should be necessary to stop the attack【170788344385436†L189-L197】.
  • If you’re unsure about the legality of your actions, call the police (110) and seek legal advice.

Ethically, violence should always be a last resort. If you feel threatened but not immediately attacked, lock the door, call for help or exit to a safer location. After any violent incident, seek psychological support (§8).

6. Entity & responsibility guide (caused / involved in trouble)

If your situation involves another form of life or responsibility (real or hypothetical), use the following decision points:

  • Accidental harm or self‑defence: See §5 for the legal framework and call emergency services if anyone is injured. Offer first aid and psychological support.
  • Biological entities (e.g., viruses, potential pregnancies): If you believe you are sick or contagious, isolate, wear a mask and contact a doctor or local health authority. For pregnancy questions (whether planned or not), talk to healthcare providers or counselling centres. They can discuss options confidentially.
  • Birth or “geschlüpft” scenarios: If a baby has just been born unexpectedly, call 112 immediately. If the environment is safe, place the newborn skin‑to‑skin on the mother’s chest and keep both warm until help arrives. For infants born days ago, ensure regular check‑ups with paediatricians and utilise state child‑support programs.
  • Long‑term parenting and responsibility: Raising a child comes with legal and social support in Germany. Contact your local Jugendamt for advice on childcare, financial support and education.
  • Silicon / technological entities: If you’re worried about an AI or machine that “escaped” or is behaving oddly, the best course is to disconnect it safely (if possible), document the behaviour and contact the manufacturer or a knowledgeable friend (“talk to the host”).
  • Ethical dilemmas: When decisions involve another life (human or not), discuss with professionals – doctors, counsellors, ethicists. Understanding the broader environmental and social context can help you make informed choices.

7. Smell remedies

Sometimes emergencies are simply olfactory. For unpleasant bathroom smells:

  • Strike a match and let it burn briefly; sulphur neutralises odours.
  • Use air freshener, scented candles or natural incense (if safe).
  • Open a window or turn on the ventilation fan.
  • Return to the calm guide (§2) and practise breathing after freshening the air.

8. Support & hotlines

When you need human contact or cannot stay in the bathroom:

  • Emergency numbers:
    • 112 – fire or medical emergencies【208495937252610†L70-L75】.
    • 110 – police emergencies【208495937252610†L96-L100】.
      Calls are free and work from any phone, even a locked mobile【208495937252610†L70-L78】. Stay on the line and follow operator instructions【208495937252610†L80-L87】. 112 works across the EU【208495937252610†L111-L119】.
  • Mental health & crisis counselling:
    • Telefonseelsorge Deutschland: 0800 1110 111 and 0800 1110 222【466149959861317†L113-L116】 – anonymous, nationwide crisis hotline.
    • International Helpline Berlin (English): 030 440 106 07【466149959861317†L106-L110】.
    • BIG Hotline (domestic violence): 030 611 0300【466149959861317†L185-L189】.
    • Violence against women support: 08000 116 016【466149959861317†L204-L205】.
    • Aids / sexual health information: various regional numbers【466149959861317†L141-L163】.
  • If you feel depressed, suicidal or overwhelmed, don’t hesitate to call. A calm voice can make all the difference.

9. Zombie & collapse guide

While a zombie outbreak is fictional, disaster preparedness is very real. The United States CDC’s “Zombie Apocalypse” campaign inspired people to think about emergencies. The following tips are adapted from preparedness guides【99681192307409†L66-L88】:

  1. Be ready: Choose a secure safe haven with barriers. Stock it with non‑perishable food, clean water, medical supplies, hygiene products, tools, radios and a bug‑out bag【99681192307409†L66-L88】.
  2. Stay informed: Monitor news and official broadcasts to learn about hazards and decide when to evacuate【99681192307409†L92-L104】.
  3. Travel light: When moving, pack only essentials (food, water, first‑aid supplies, simple tools)【99681192307409†L107-L116】.
  4. Be quiet: Avoid loud noises that attract unwanted attention; close doors quietly and opt for walking over noisy machines【99681192307409†L118-L127】.
  5. Learn self‑defence: Practise basic self‑defence and safe use of tools or improvised weapons【99681192307409†L130-L138】.
  6. Create a community: Form groups with like‑minded people; cooperate on food, defence and decision‑making【99681192307409†L140-L152】.
  7. Maintain hygiene: Wash hands regularly, disinfect surfaces and wear masks or gloves to prevent infection【99681192307409†L156-L166】.
  8. Be adaptable: Be ready to change plans, improvise and follow local policies during crises【99681192307409†L169-L179】.
  9. Stay optimistic: Focus on what you can control and encourage those around you【99681192307409†L182-L191】.
  10. Keep moving: Don’t stay in one place too long; move quickly and cautiously, avoid drawing attention and have navigation tools【99681192307409†L193-L203】.

Whether the emergency involves zombies, storms or power cuts, these preparedness steps improve resilience.

10. Closing thoughts

This guide is meant to empower you to handle small and large emergencies with a clear head and a touch of humour. You are allowed to take breaks, laugh and ask for help. Trust yourself, follow the flowchart and remember: when in doubt, breathe.
