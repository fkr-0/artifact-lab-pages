\section{How to Use This Guide}\label{how-to-use-this-guide}

\subsection{Purpose}\label{purpose}

This document is a decision-support tool designed for deployment in a
bathroom environment. It provides structured guidance for situations
ranging from acute anxiety and physical pain to --- hypothetically ---
zombie apocalypses. Every path through this guide converges on one of
four practical destinations: the \textbf{Calm Guide} (→ Ch.4), the
\textbf{Self Ambulance Guide} (→ Ch.5), the \textbf{Zombie Guide} (→
Ch.6), or the \textbf{Professional Support Directory} (→ Ch.7).
Regardless of where you enter, you will always be routed toward
actionable next steps.

The guide's architecture is formally a directed acyclic graph (DAG) ---
a structure where information flows in one direction and never loops
back on itself (except where explicitly marked 🔁). This means you can
enter at any point and still reach a destination without getting stuck
in circular reasoning, which is more than most anxious brains can claim.

\subsection{Target Audience}\label{target-audience}

Anyone currently in a bathroom who needs guidance --- emotional,
medical, ethical, or existential. No prerequisites beyond physical
presence and a willingness to read. The guide assumes no prior training
in first aid, psychology, or zombie survival, though it helps if you can
operate a door lock.

\subsection{Guide Topology --- A Graph-Theoretic
Model}\label{guide-topology-a-graph-theoretic-model}

For the mathematically inclined (or the analytically anxious who need to
understand the structure before trusting it), this guide can be
formalized as a directed graph:

\[G = (V, E)\]

where \(V\) is the set of nodes and \(E\) is the set of directed edges.
Our guide topology has:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Entry nodes} (\(|V_{entry}| = 7\)): Situations A through G ---
  the seven ways you arrived in this bathroom
\item
  \textbf{Decision nodes} (\(|V_{decision}| \approx 14\)): Internal ◆
  points within each situation
\item
  \textbf{Destination nodes} (\(|V_{dest}| = 4\)): The four destination
  guides (Calm, Self Ambulance, Zombie, Professional Support)
\end{itemize}

The total graph: \(|V| = 25\) nodes, \(|E| \approx 38\) directed edges.
Every path from an entry node reaches at least one destination node ---
this is the graph's \textbf{reachability invariant}, and it's what
guarantees you'll never get stuck in this guide without a next
step.\footnote{The graph-theoretic framing is more than decorative.
  Decision trees are a well-studied structure in operations research and
  decision analysis (Raiffa, 1968). The reachability invariant --- every
  path reaches a destination --- is a formal property that guarantees no
  ``dead end'' states exist in the guide. For readers who want to go
  deeper, see: Howard, R.A. \& Matheson, J.E. ``Influence diagrams''
  (1981), and the broader field of Bayesian decision networks. The graph
  can also be analyzed as a Markov chain, where transition probabilities
  depend on the reader's emotional state --- but that's a research
  paper, not a bathroom guide.}

\subsection{Your Current Status}\label{your-current-status}

You are in a bathroom. By definition:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  ✓ The door likely locks --- you have privacy
\item
  ✓ Running water is available --- you have hygiene resources
\item
  ✓ You are indoors --- you are sheltered
\item
  ✓ You chose to come here --- some part of you is already practicing
  self-care
\end{itemize}

Let's formalize your resource state. Define \(R\) as the set of
available resources:

\[R = \{privacy, water, shelter, self\_awareness\}\]

With \(|R| = 4\), you have more resources than your anxiety is letting
you see. Anxiety narrows the perceived resource set to
\(|R_{perceived}| \ll |R|\). The purpose of this guide is to restore
\(R_{perceived} \rightarrow R\). This is not metaphor --- it's cognitive
restructuring, and it works.

This is a stable starting position. Proceed with whatever brought you
here.

\section{Situation A --- ``I Caused Trouble (Involving a
Life/Entity)''}\label{situation-a-i-caused-trouble-involving-a-lifeentity}

So you've affected another sensible form of life --- or something that
might become one. First: the fact that you're reading this in a bathroom
suggests you haven't fled the country, which is already a positive sign.
Let's sort out what exactly happened.

\textbf{Step 1. Identify the nature of the entity.}

\begin{verbatim}
◆ What kind of entity are we talking about?

    ├─ Biological (virus, bacterium, multicellular…)
    │     └─ Does it cause zombification?
    │           ├─ Yes → 🔁 Re-classify as "post-apocalypse" → 📍 Zombie Guide (Ch.6)
    │           └─ No → Proceed to Step 2
    │
    └─ Silicon-based (AI, software, nanotech…)
          └─ Did it escape containment?
                ├─ Yes → Is it BSI (Bundesamt für Sicherheit in der
                │        Informationstechnik) territory?
                │     ├─ Yes → Contact BSI. Seriously. Right now.
                │     │        Then → 📍 Calm Guide (Ch.4)
                │     └─ No  → How does it feel about being loose?
                │           ├─ Good → Hmm. Talk to your host about
                │           │        this interesting situation.
                │           │        Then → 📍 Calm Guide (Ch.4)
                │           └─ Bad  → See Biological + "Geschlüpft"
                │                     (it's alive and angry — treat as
                │                      biological emergency)
                └─ No → Cool. Containment holds.
                       → Monitor + 📍 Calm Guide (Ch.4)
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{A1 --- Created a Life Form · Not Yet
``Geschlüpft''}\label{a1-created-a-life-form-not-yet-geschluxfcpft}

The entity exists in potential but has not independently emerged. You
are at a decision point with a time window.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Assess the timeline.} How long until the situation changes
  irreversibly? Write it down if you need to think clearly. The
  gestation table above gives you the medical milestones; your personal
  timeline may have its own deadlines (legal windows, personal
  readiness, financial stability).
\item
  \textbf{Medical support.} Consult a medical professional within the
  available timeframe. This is not a decision this bathroom guide can or
  should make for you --- but it \emph{is} a decision that benefits from
  professional input. Point yourself toward: your gynecologist, a family
  planning center (Pro Familia in Germany, similar organizations
  elsewhere), or the nearest hospital.
\item
  \textbf{Abortion yes/no?} This is your decision. No flowchart resolves
  it. What this guide \emph{can} do is point you toward people trained
  to help you think it through without judgment → 📍 Professional
  Support Directory (Ch.7, §14.4).
\item
  \textbf{Ethical note.} Whatever you decide, you are allowed to have
  feelings about it. Those feelings are also worth bringing to a
  professional → 📍 Calm Guide (Ch.4) if you need to steady yourself
  first. Decision-making under acute stress degrades cognitive function
  --- research shows that cortisol levels above baseline significantly
  impair prefrontal cortex activity, which is exactly the brain region
  you need for this kind of deliberation.\footnote{Arnsten, A.F.T.
    ``Stress signalling pathways that impair prefrontal cortex structure
    and function.'' \emph{Nature Reviews Neuroscience} 10, 410--422
    (2009). Cortisol impairs dendritic branching in prefrontal cortex
    neurons, degrading the working memory and executive function needed
    for complex decisions. This is why major decisions under acute
    stress are reliably worse than those made in calmer states.}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{A2 --- Created a Life Form · ``Geschlüpft'' · Minutes
Ago}\label{a2-created-a-life-form-geschluxfcpft-minutes-ago}

Congratulations and/or condolences --- depending on context, possibly
both simultaneously.

\begin{verbatim}
◆ Apocalypse status?

    ├─ Post-apocalypse? ──→ 📍 Zombie Guide (Ch.6)
    │                      + 📍 Self Ambulance Guide (Ch.5)
    │                      ("Self Ambulance Healing" — you're it)
    │
    └─ Pre-apocalypse? ──→ ◆ Are other humans available?
                              ├─ Yes → Call 110. Now.
                              │        Find other humans immediately.
                              │        You need support, not heroics.
                              └─ No  → 📍 Self Ambulance Guide (Ch.5)
                                       + 📍 Calm Guide (Ch.4)
                                       (You're doing emergency
                                        self-sufficiency. Breathe first.)
\end{verbatim}

\textbf{Key principle:} Minutes-old situations are time-critical. Act
before you overthink. Overthinking is a luxury available in A4 and
beyond. Your sympathetic nervous system is in overdrive --- that's by
design (fight-or-flight exists for a reason) --- but it means your
decision-making is running on the amygdala, not the prefrontal cortex.
If you can, execute these steps \emph{before} the adrenaline crash hits.

\subsection{A3 --- Created a Life Form · ``Geschlüpft'' · Days
Ago}\label{a3-created-a-life-form-geschluxfcpft-days-ago}

The immediate crisis has passed. What remains is the entirety of
everything else.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Medical support.} If not already done: contact ambulance
  services if needed, and locate a local pediatrician / children's
  doctor. The first days of a new life have specific medical checkpoints
  --- don't skip them → 📍 Professional Support Directory (Ch.7, §14.4).
  Newborn screening (the ``U2'' and ``U3'' examinations in Germany)
  should occur within the first 3--10 days and again at 4--5 weeks
  respectively.
\item
  \textbf{The ethical dimension.} Here's the thing: nature's tendency to
  reproduce is deeply non-logical. It doesn't reason. It just\ldots{}
  proliferates. The fact that you're reading this in a bathroom suggests
  you're doing something nature doesn't --- you're \emph{thinking} about
  it. That's a fundamentally human act. Talk to someone about this. Not
  the bathroom. A person → 📍 Professional Support Directory (Ch.7,
  §14.2).
\item
  \textbf{Environmental lens.} If your mind is spiraling toward resource
  scarcity, environmental decay, and whether bringing another human onto
  this planet is defensible --- those are valid concerns. They deserve
  engagement, not suppression. Recommended reading: any serious climate
  ethics text, and then → 📍 Calm Guide (Ch.4) to process before making
  any irreversible decisions.
\end{enumerate}

The entity is now a walking, talking, occasionally unreasonable small

\subsection{A5 --- Created a Life Form · \textgreater{} 20 Years
Ago}\label{a5-created-a-life-form-20-years-ago}

They're an adult. You've done your shift. Grow up, pls --- and we say
that with warmth.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Reframe.} Your creation is now an autonomous agent with their
  own decision-making apparatus (questionable as it may seem at times).
  Your responsibility has transitioned from ``keeping them alive'' to
  ``being available if they ask.'' Formally: your duty function \(D(t)\)
  transitions from \(D_{active}\) to \(D_{available}\) around the time
  they start paying their own rent.
\item
  \textbf{Look at ``friends.''} If you're struggling with this
  transition, the issue may not be them --- it may be you. That's not a
  criticism; it's an observation. → 📍 Calm Guide (Ch.4) for immediate
  grounding, → 📍 Professional Support Directory (Ch.7, §14.2) for
  deeper work.
\item
  \textbf{Practical advice.} Call them. Not to fix anything. Just to
  talk. If that's hard, the Calm Guide has conversation strategies.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{A6 --- Created a Life Form · Ambiguous /
Otherwise}\label{a6-created-a-life-form-ambiguous-otherwise}

The timeline is unclear, the situation is messy, or it doesn't fit
neatly into A1--A5. Welcome to the majority of human experience.

\textbf{Sub-path (a): Don't want to, but will.} You're being pulled
toward something by obligation, expectation, or inertia. That tension is
real. Acknowledge it → 📍 Calm Guide (Ch.4) to reduce the noise, then

\textbf{Step 3. Optional: Name it to tame it.}

There's genuine psychological research behind this: verbally labeling an
emotion reduces its intensity through a process called ``affect

\subsubsection{The Stress Response: What Your Body Is
Doing}\label{the-stress-response-what-your-body-is-doing}

When you feel endangered, your sympathetic nervous system activates the
fight-or-flight response. This isn't a malfunction --- it's an ancient
survival system. Understanding it helps you ride the wave instead of
drowning in it:

\[S(t) = S_0 + A \cdot (1 - e^{-\alpha t})\]

Where \(S(t)\) is your sympathetic activation level at time \(t\),
\(S_0\) is baseline, \(A\) is the amplitude of the threat response, and
\(\alpha\) is the activation rate. The key insight: this curve rises
fast but decays slowly. Your adrenaline spikes in seconds, but cortisol
--- the stress hormone that keeps you on high alert --- has a half-life
of 60--90 minutes.\footnote{Taves MD, et al.~``Cortisol half-life and
  clearance: clinical implications.'' \emph{Journal of Clinical
  Endocrinology \& Metabolism} (2011). Cortisol plasma half-life:
  \textasciitilde60--90 minutes. Active metabolites persist longer. This
  is why the ``shakes'' after a threat can last 1--2 hours --- your
  biochemistry hasn't caught up to your reality.} Social-evaluative
threats and uncontrollable stressors produce the largest cortisol
responses,\footnote{Dickerson, S.S. \& Kemeny, M.E. ``Acute stressors
  and cortisol responses: a meta-analytic integration.''
  \emph{Psychological Bulletin} 130(5), 733--756 (2004).
  Social-evaluative threats produce the largest cortisol responses;
  uncontrollable stressors produce larger responses than controllable
  ones. Being trapped in a bathroom with no exit strategy is,
  tragically, both.} which is unfortunately exactly what being trapped
in a bathroom with no exit strategy feels like. This is why you'll feel
shaky and hypervigilant long after the danger passes. That's not
weakness; that's biochemistry.

\textbf{Step 2. Resource scan.}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Talk to the host.} If you're in someone else's space, the host
  is your ally. They know the environment, the exits, and the social
  dynamics. Ask for help --- it's not weakness, it's tactical
  intelligence gathering.
\item
  \textbf{Self-defense: know the law before you need it.} In Germany,
  §32 StGB (Notwehr) covers self-defense: you may defend yourself
  against an unlawful attack, using force proportionate to the threat.
  The four legal elements are: (1) present attack, (2) unlawfulness, (3)
  defensive action, (4) proportionality.\footnote{Strafgesetzbuch (StGB)
    §32 Notwehr. The four-element framework is derived from the
    legislative text and consistent German Federal Court (BGH)
    jurisprudence. See also §33 (Excessive self-defence) for cases where
    the proportionality threshold was exceeded under conditions of
    confusion, fear, or terror.} Know this \emph{before} you need it →
  📍 Professional Support Directory (Ch.7, §14.3).
\item

  available? Keys (pointy), phone (communication + flashlight), aerosol
  cans (improvised deterrent), heavy objects (strategic, not
  recreational). You're not MacGyver --- you're just aware.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Cognitive Load Theory --- Why Your Brain Is
Crashing}\label{cognitive-load-theory-why-your-brain-is-crashing}

George Miller's famous 1956 paper proposed that working memory can hold
approximately \(7 \pm 2\) items simultaneously.\footnote{Miller, G.A.
  ``The magical number seven, plus or minus two: some limits on our
  capacity for processing information.'' \emph{Psychological Review}
  63(2), 81--97 (1956). One of the most cited papers in psychology.
  Miller himself thought the ``7±2'' framing was slightly
  tongue-in-cheek --- it became iconic anyway.} More recent research by
Cowan (2001) refines this to approximately \(4 \pm 1\) chunks of
information.\footnote{Cowan, N. ``The magical number 4 in short-term
  memory: a reconsideration of mental storage capacity.''
  \emph{Behavioral and Brain Sciences} 24(1), 87--114 (2001). The
  refined estimate accounts for chunking strategies --- trained
  individuals can hold more by grouping items into larger chunks.} When
you're ``congesting,'' you're exceeding this capacity --- your cognitive
buffer is overflowing.

\[\text{Cognitive Load} = \text{Intrinsic} + \text{Extraneous} + \text{Germane}\]

Where intrinsic load comes from the task itself, extraneous load comes
from poor presentation or unnecessary information, and germane load is
the productive effort of learning/processing. Congestion happens when
the total exceeds your working memory capacity. The fix: reduce
extraneous load (turn off notifications, close tabs, delegate) and
process intrinsic load one chunk at a time. This is literally what the
``top 3 things'' exercise does --- it moves items from working memory to
external storage (paper/phone), freeing up cognitive bandwidth.

\subsubsection{The Chemistry of Match-Based Odor
Neutralization}\label{the-chemistry-of-match-based-odor-neutralization}

The SO₂ mechanism is real and surprisingly elegant. When a match is
struck, the combustion of sulfur in the match head produces sulfur
dioxide (\(SO_2\)), which is a strong electrophile. Many malodorous
compounds --- particularly volatile organic compounds (VOCs) and
hydrogen sulfide (\(H_2S\)) --- are nucleophilic. The \(SO_2\) reacts
with these molecules, altering their molecular structure and thereby
changing (or eliminating) their odor profile.\footnote{The SO₂ odor
  neutralization mechanism is widely documented in chemistry education
  but poorly studied in controlled settings (perhaps because funding
  agencies don't prioritize ``bathroom match'' research). The
  electrophilic-nucleophilic interaction is basic organic chemistry; the
  effect on volatile sulfur compounds (VSCs) like H₂S and CH₃SH is
  documented in atmospheric chemistry literature. See: Atkinson, R.
  ``Gas-phase tropospheric chemistry of organic compounds.''
  \emph{Journal of Physical and Chemical Reference Data} (1994).} The
effect is chemical neutralization, not mere masking --- which is why
matches work better than perfume for bathroom odors. The concentration
of \(SO_2\) from a single match is tiny and safe in ventilated spaces,
though we don't recommend huffing match heads. Everything in moderation,
including chemistry.

This is the only entry point where the solution might genuinely be
``light a match and move on.'' Enjoy it. The other sections are heavier.

This guide is referenced by almost every path in this document. That's

for solving anything. You can't navigate a decision tree while your
nervous system is running kernel panic. Let's get you to a stable state
first, then figure out what's next.

\subsection{You're Allowed to Be Here}\label{youre-allowed-to-be-here}

\textbf{Permission statement.} Lock the door. Sit down. You are in a
bathroom, which means you are in a room designed specifically for
private bodily maintenance. Your brain is a body part. You are
maintaining it. This is not avoidance --- this is triage.

\textbf{The 10-minute rule.} The next 10 minutes are officially
\textbf{Me Time™}. Not because your problems aren't real, but because
your nervous system needs a reset window before it can process anything
useful. Think of it as rebooting in safe mode. Nothing dramatic happens
in safe mode. That's the point.

We can formalize this. The sympathetic-parasympathetic transition ---
the shift from ``fight-or-flight'' to ``rest-and-digest'' --- has a
characteristic time constant. Research on autonomic recovery shows that
parasympathetic re-engagement begins within approximately 10 minutes of
stressor removal:\footnote{Mezzacappa, E.S., et al.~``Vagal rebound and
  recovery from psychological stress.'' \emph{Psychosomatic Medicine}
  63(4), 650--657 (2001). Parasympathetic (vagal) re-engagement is
  observable within 5--10 minutes of stressor offset, with full recovery
  typically requiring 20--60 minutes depending on stressor intensity and
  individual vagal tone.}

\[t_{reset} \approx 10\text{min}\]

This isn't arbitrary. It reflects the time required for vagal tone to
reassert dominance over sympathetic activation. Your heart rate
variability (HRV) --- a key marker of parasympathetic activity ---
begins to recover within this window. The 10-minute rule isn't
self-indulgence; it's neurology.

\textbf{Where you are, factually:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Indoors ✓ (weather is not your problem right now)
\item
  Seated or can sit ✓ (gravity: manageable)
\item
  Door locks ✓ (privacy: available)
\item
  Running water ✓ (hydration + hygiene: accessible)
\item
  Reading this ✓ (cognitive function: operational, even if barely)
\end{itemize}

You have more resources than your anxiety is letting you see. That's
what anxiety does --- it narrows the aperture. We're widening it back
up.

\subsection{Stress Decay Curve}\label{stress-decay-curve}

Your stress level isn't static --- it follows a decay function once the
stressor is removed or you begin active calming. The cortisol decay
model describes this elegantly:

\[C(t) = C_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda t}\]

Where \(C(t)\) is your cortisol level at time \(t\), \(C_0\) is the peak
cortisol level, and \(\lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{t_{1/2}}\) is the decay
constant. With a cortisol half-life of approximately 60--90
minutes:\footnote{Taves MD, et al.~``Cortisol half-life and clearance.''
  \emph{J Clin Endocrinol Metab} (2011). Plasma cortisol half-life:
  \textasciitilde60--90 minutes. The decay is approximately exponential,
  making the \(C(t) = C_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda t}\) model a reasonable
  approximation. Active relaxation techniques increase clearance rate by
  enhancing hepatic metabolism and reducing HPA axis drive.}

\[\lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{75\text{min}} \approx 0.0092 \text{ min}^{-1}\]

This means: after 75 minutes, your cortisol has dropped by half. After
150 minutes, it's at 25\%. The curve is exponential --- fast initial
drop, then gradual return to baseline. Active calming (breathing,
grounding) accelerates this process by boosting parasympathetic
activity, effectively increasing \(\lambda\). You can't wish cortisol
away, but you can help your body clear it faster.

\subsubsection{Technique 1: Box Breathing
(4-4-4-4)}\label{technique-1-box-breathing-4-4-4-4}

Used by Navy SEALs, which means it works under conditions significantly
worse than ``sitting in a bathroom.'' The geometry is simple:

\begin{verbatim}
        4 seconds
    ┌──────────────┐
    │              │
    │   INHALE     │ HOLD
    │              │
4   │              │   4
sec │              │ sec
    │              │
    │  EXHALE      │ HOLD
    │              │
    └──────────────┘
        4 seconds
\end{verbatim}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  Inhale through the nose for 4 counts.
\item
  Hold for 4 counts.
\item
  Exhale through the mouth for 4 counts.
\item
  Hold for 4 counts.
\item
  Repeat 4 cycles (total: \textasciitilde64 seconds). Don't rush the
  counts. If 4 is too long, use 3. If 4 is too easy, use 5. The box
  shape matters more than the number.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Efficacy data:} Box breathing increases HRV by approximately
15--25\% within 5 minutes of practice, indicating a measurable shift
toward parasympathetic dominance.\footnote{Laborde, S., et al.~``Heart
  rate variability and cardiac vagal tone in psychophysiological
  research.'' \emph{Frontiers in Psychology} 8, 213 (2017). Slow-paced
  breathing (5.5--6 breaths/min) produces the largest HRV increases, but
  even 4-4-4-4 box breathing at \textasciitilde4 breaths/min shows
  significant effects within 5 minutes.} HRV --- heart rate variability
--- is the gold-standard non-invasive marker of autonomic balance,
defined as:

\[HRV = \frac{SD_{RR}}{\overline{RR}}\]

Where \(SD_{RR}\) is the standard deviation of RR intervals
(beat-to-beat distances) and \(\overline{RR}\) is the mean interval.
Higher HRV = more parasympathetic activity = calmer nervous system. When
you breathe slowly and rhythmically, you're literally increasing the
mathematical variability of your heartbeat, which paradoxically means
your heart is \emph{more} adaptable and resilient.

\subsubsection{Technique 2: 4-7-8
Breathing}\label{technique-2-4-7-8-breathing}

A slightly more aggressive calming technique. Good for when box
breathing feels too structured and you just need to slow down.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  Inhale through the nose for 4 counts.
\item
  Hold for 7 counts.
\item
  Exhale through the mouth (audibly) for 8 counts.
\item
  Repeat 3 cycles. If you feel lightheaded, return to normal breathing
  --- you've done enough.
\end{enumerate}

The extended exhale is the key mechanism. Exhalation activates the vagus
nerve (your parasympathetic superhighway), which is why longer
exhale-than-inhale patterns are reliably calming. The 7-count hold
allows CO₂ to build slightly, which paradoxically improves oxygen
delivery to tissues via the Bohr effect --- your hemoglobin releases O₂
more readily in slightly more acidic (higher CO₂)
environments.\footnote{The Bohr effect (Christian Bohr, 1904):
  hemoglobin's oxygen-binding affinity is inversely related to both
  acidity and CO₂ concentration. Slightly elevated CO₂ (from
  breath-holding) causes hemoglobin to release O₂ more readily to
  tissues. This is why controlled breath-holds don't reduce oxygen
  delivery --- they enhance it. The effect is small but real, and it's
  the physiological basis for therapeutic breath-hold practices.}

\subsubsection{Technique 3: Physiological Sigh (Emergency
Use)}\label{technique-3-physiological-sigh-emergency-use}

Discovered by Dr.~Andrew Huberman's lab at Stanford, this is the fastest
known way to reduce real-time physiological arousal --- and the only
breathing technique with a peer-reviewed RCT demonstrating real-time
stress reduction.\footnote{Balban, M.Y., et al.~``Brief structured
  respiration practices enhance mood and reduce physiological arousal.''
  \emph{Cell Reports Medicine} 4(1), 100895 (2023). This randomized
  controlled trial (n=114) compared cyclic physiological sighing, box
  breathing, cyclic hyperventilation, and mindfulness meditation over 28
  days. Cyclic physiological sighing produced the greatest improvement
  in mood and reduction in respiratory rate. The technique involves a
  double inhale through the nose followed by an extended exhale through
  the mouth.}

\begin{verbatim}
    Inhale  →  Inhale  →  Exhale
    (short)    (top-up)    (long)
    ···→       ·→          ←←←←←←
\end{verbatim}

Two quick inhales through the nose, one long exhale through the mouth.
Even one round works. Do 2--3 if you can.

\textbf{The mechanism:} The double inhale reinflates collapsed alveoli
(the tiny air sacs in your lungs), dramatically increasing the surface
area available for CO₂ exchange. On the long exhale, you offload CO₂ at
an accelerated rate:

\[\text{CO}_2\text{ offload rate} \propto \frac{A_{alveoli} \cdot \Delta P_{CO_2}}{d_{membrane}}\]

Where \(A_{alveoli}\) is the total alveolar surface area (doubled by the
second inhale), \(\Delta P_{CO_2}\) is the CO₂ partial pressure
gradient, and \(d_{membrane}\) is the diffusion distance across the
alveolar-capillary membrane. More surface area + maintained gradient =
faster CO₂ clearance = faster heart rate reduction = faster calm. Your
heart rate drops. It's biology, not magic --- but it works like magic.

The Huberman Lab study showed that just 5 minutes of cyclic
physiological sighing produced greater reductions in physiological
arousal than 5 minutes of mindfulness meditation, making it the most
efficient single technique for acute stress.\footnote{Balban, M.Y., et
  al.~``Brief structured respiration practices enhance mood and reduce
  physiological arousal.'' \emph{Cell Reports Medicine} 4(1), 100895
  (2023). This randomized controlled trial (n=114) compared cyclic
  physiological sighing, box breathing, cyclic hyperventilation, and
  mindfulness meditation over 28 days. Cyclic physiological sighing
  produced the greatest improvement in mood and reduction in respiratory
  rate. The technique involves a double inhale through the nose followed
  by an extended exhale through the mouth.}

counteract distress.'' It's not self-indulgence; it's pharmacology you
can do without a prescription.

The specificity is key. ``I need help'' is hard to respond to. ``Can you
sit with me for 10 minutes'' is easy.

\section{🏥 Self Ambulance Guide --- You Are the First Responder
(Because You're the Only One
Here)}\label{self-ambulance-guide-you-are-the-first-responder-because-youre-the-only-one-here}

The premise of this guide is simple: professional help is not yet
present, and something needs doing. ``Self ambulance'' doesn't mean you
replace doctors --- it means you stabilize the patient (you) until
doctors can take over. Think of yourself as the adhesive bandage of the
medical world: temporary, essential, and way better than nothing.

\subsection{Assess: Life-Threatening?}\label{assess-life-threatening}

Before anything else, run this triage. It takes 10 seconds.

\begin{verbatim}
◆ Is this immediately life-threatening?

    Signs that say YES:
    ─ Uncontrolled bleeding (sputing / soaking through fabric)
    ─ Difficulty breathing or no breathing
    ─ Loss of consciousness, unresponsive
    ─ Chest pain + jaw/arm pain + sweating + nausea
    ─ Seizure in progress
    ─ Severe head injury with confusion or vomiting
    ─ Suspected spinal injury (numbness, can't move limbs)

    ├─ YES → Call 112 immediately (or 110 if police needed).
    │         Do not finish reading this guide first.
    │         Speakerphone is your friend — dial, then follow
    │         dispatcher instructions while keeping hands free.
    │
    └─ NO → Continue to Basic First Aid.
\end{verbatim}

\textbf{The 10-second rule:} If you're unsure whether it's
life-threatening, treat it as if it is. Call 112. Dispatchers would
rather talk you through a false alarm than not get the call for a real
one. You are not wasting their time. That's literally what they're there
for.

The heatmap concept can also be formalized using Bayes' theorem for
triage decisions --- calculating the posterior probability that a
symptom constellation represents a life-threatening condition given the
observed signs:\footnote{Gill, C.J., et al.~``A method for Bayesian
  triage in resource-limited settings.'' \emph{PLoS ONE} (various).
  While clinicians rarely calculate explicit Bayes' theorem, the
  underlying reasoning --- ``how likely is this to be serious given what
  I'm seeing?'' --- is Bayesian inference. The key insight for
  self-triage: when in doubt, assume higher severity (prioritize
  sensitivity over specificity). A false alarm is cheap; a missed
  emergency is catastrophic.}

\[P(\text{critical} \mid \text{signs}) = \frac{P(\text{signs} \mid \text{critical}) \cdot P(\text{critical})}{P(\text{signs})}\]

You don't need to calculate this --- your brain does an approximate
version automatically when it says ``this feels really bad.'' Trust that
intuition when it pushes you toward P1. The false-positive cost (an
unnecessary 112 call) is vastly lower than the false-negative cost (not
calling when you should have).

\subsubsection{Wounds}\label{wounds}

\begin{verbatim}
◆ Is the wound bleeding?

    ├─ Heavily (soaking through cloth in < 30 seconds)
    │     1. Apply firm, direct pressure with clean cloth.
    │        Do NOT remove the first cloth if it soaks through —
    │        add more on top. Removing it breaks the clot.
    │     2. Elevate the wound above heart level if possible.
    │     3. If limb: apply a tourniquet ONLY if bleeding is
    │        life-threatening and pressure isn't working.
    │        Note the time you applied it. Tell the paramedics.
    │     4. Call 112 if not already done.
    │
    ├─ Moderately (oozing, slow)
    │     1. Clean under cool running water.
    │     2. Apply pressure for 5–10 minutes without peeking.
    │     3. Cover with a sterile bandage or clean cloth.
    │     4. Monitor for signs of infection over next 48 hours:
    │        redness spreading, warmth, red streaks, fever.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Don't move it.} The body part is in the position it's in for a

\item

  does not have an X-ray machine (and if it does, we have different
  questions).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{When to Call for Help}\label{when-to-call-for-help}

\subsubsection{Call 112 immediately if:}\label{call-112-immediately-if}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Uncontrolled bleeding that doesn't slow with 10 minutes of pressure
\item
  Difficulty breathing or chest pain with jaw/arm radiation
\item
  Loss of consciousness (even brief)
\item
  Suspected stroke: face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty
  (remember: \textbf{FAST} --- Face, Arms, Speech, Time)
\item
  Severe allergic reaction: swelling of throat/tongue, difficulty
  breathing, widespread hives
\item
  Seizure lasting \textgreater{} 5 minutes or first-time seizure
\item
  Head injury with vomiting, confusion, or loss of consciousness
\item
  Severe abdominal pain with rigidity (stomach hard as a board)
\item
  Any situation where you think ``I should probably call'' --- call
\end{itemize}

\textbf{The golden rule:} If you're debating whether to call, the debate
itself is a signal. Call. Dispatcher \textgreater{} regret.

\subsection{Self-Care While Waiting}\label{self-care-while-waiting}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Position yourself safely.} Conscious and breathing fine? Sit
  or lie in a comfortable position. Semi-recumbent (propped up at 45°)
  works well. Feeling faint? Lie flat with legs elevated. Nauseous? Lie
  on your side (recovery position). The bathroom floor is a perfectly
  acceptable place for this.
\item
  \textbf{Stay conscious.} If you feel yourself fading, fight it --- not
  heroically, just practically: talk to yourself out loud, keep your
  eyes open and focused on a specific object, move your fingers and
  toes. Sensory input keeps the brain online.
\item
  \textbf{Breathe.} → 📍 Calm Guide (Ch.4, Breathing Exercises). The
  same breathing techniques that help anxiety also help pain and shock.
\item
  \textbf{Don't eat or drink.} If you might need surgery, an empty
  stomach is safer. Small sips of water are usually fine, but ask the
  dispatcher.
\item
  \textbf{Unlock the door.} If you've called 112, emergency services
  need to be able to reach you. Unlock the bathroom door now, while you
  still can.
\item
  \textbf{Phone placement.} Put your phone on speaker or place it within
  arm's reach. Don't hold it in your hand if you might lose
  consciousness --- it'll fall and become unreachable.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Survival Probability
Function}\label{survival-probability-function}

Before we dive into the practical stuff, let's establish the
mathematical framework. Your survival probability over time follows an
exponential decay model --- not because you're doomed, but because risk
accumulates:

\[P(\text{survive}, t) = e^{-\lambda t}\]

Where \(\lambda\) is the hazard rate (probability of a fatal event per
unit time). The good news: \(\lambda\) is not fixed. Every skill you
acquire, every resource you secure, every ally you find \emph{reduces}
\(\lambda\). The survival function becomes:

\[P(\text{survive}, t) = e^{-(\lambda_0 - \sum_{i} r_i) t}\]

Where \(\lambda_0\) is the baseline hazard and each \(r_i\) is a risk
reduction factor: water secured, shelter established, group formed,
perimeter defended. The math says: every preparation matters. Every
skill moves the curve. You are not helpless --- you're a variable in
your own survival equation.\footnote{The exponential survival model
  \(P(t) = e^{-\lambda t}\) is the foundation of survival analysis in
  statistics and reliability engineering. It assumes a constant hazard
  rate, which is a simplification --- real hazard rates change with
  environment, season, and preparation level. The modified model
  \(P(t) = e^{-(\lambda_0 - \sum r_i)t}\) is a proportional hazards
  approximation (Cox, 1972). Each risk reduction factor \(r_i\)
  effectively shifts the entire survival curve upward.}

\subsubsection{Food}\label{food}

You can survive \textasciitilde3 weeks without food. That's generous
compared to water, but your cognitive function degrades fast without
calories.

\textbf{Caloric requirements:}

\[E_{basal} \approx 1500 \text{ kcal/day} \quad \text{(resting metabolic rate)}\]
\[E_{survival} \approx 2500\text{–}3000 \text{ kcal/day} \quad \text{(active survival activity)}\]

The gap between basal and active survival requirements
(\(\Delta E \approx 1000\text{–}1500\) kcal) is the energy you spend on
foraging, building, defending, and moving. Every unnecessary activity
burns calories you can't afford to waste.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Foraging.} Learn 3--5 easily identifiable local wild plants
  that are edible. Dandelion (entire plant), nettles (cook first),
  blackberries, wild garlic --- widespread across Europe. If you can't
  identify it with certainty, don't eat it. Starvation is preferable to
  poisoning.
\item
  \textbf{Hunting/trapping.} Small game (rabbits, birds) is more
  practical than large game. Snares require wire and knowledge. Fishing
  requires water and patience. Principle: calories-in \textgreater{}
  calories-out. Don't spend more energy catching food than the food
  provides.
\item
  \textbf{Scavenging.} In a collapse scenario, preserved food in
  abandoned stores is your friend. Canned goods last years. Check seals
  --- bulging cans are bacterial, not food. Prioritize: canned
  \textgreater{} dried \textgreater{} fresh (perishable).
\item
  \textbf{Insects.} Yes, really. Crickets, grubs, ants --- high protein,
  widely available, no hunting skill required. Cook them if you can. If
  you can't, they're still more nutritious than your pride.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Shelter}\label{shelter}

Exposure kills faster than starvation. Hypothermia: wet + cold + wind.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Location.} High ground (avoid flood zones), away from obvious
  paths (avoid detection), near water source but not directly beside it.
\item
  \textbf{Insulation.} Layers of dry leaves, pine needles, cardboard,
  clothing --- anything between you and the ground. The ground is a heat
  sink.
\item
  \textbf{Windbreak.} A wall, hedge, car, or stacked debris blocking the
  prevailing wind direction. Wind strips heat. Block it.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Reading Traces \& Observing
Environments}\label{reading-traces-observing-environments}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Tracks.} Disturbed dirt, broken twigs, bent grass ---
  direction and recency. Fresh tracks have sharp edges; old ones are
  rounded by wind and rain.
\item
  \textbf{Sound.} Learn the baseline soundscape. Anything that breaks
  the baseline is information. Birds alarm-calling = predator nearby.
\item
  \textbf{Smell.} Smoke = fire = other humans (or uncontrolled
  wildfire). Rotting = biological hazard. Cooking food = people,
  potentially friendly.
\item
  \textbf{Patterns.} Zombies (canonical model) are attracted to sound
  and movement. Move quietly, deliberately, in shadow when possible.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Energy}\label{energy}

Conserve it. The survival economy is brutally simple:

\begin{verbatim}
  Energy in (food) ──→ Energy out (activity)

  Rule: If the activity doesn't contribute to survival,
        don't do it. Panic running is energy waste.
        Strategic walking is energy investment.
\end{verbatim}

\subsubsection{Hygiene}\label{hygiene}

The thing that kills most people in disaster scenarios isn't the
disaster itself --- it's the infection that follows. Cholera, dysentery,
and wound sepsis have killed more humans than every zombie movie
combined.

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Defecate} away from water sources and camp. Bury it. Minimum
  30m from water, 30m from shelter.
\item
  \textbf{Wash hands} with ash and water if soap is unavailable. Ash is
  alkaline and breaks down oils/bacteria.
\item
  \textbf{Wounds} must be cleaned immediately. Even small cuts can
  become lethal in a low-hygiene environment.
\item
  \textbf{Teeth.} If you find a toothbrush, it's more valuable than you
  think. Improvise: chewed stick (miswak technique), salt water rinse.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Securing Friends + People in

\item
  \textbf{Rescue those who need it.} Children, elderly, injured,
  isolated --- these people cost resources short-term and provide social
  cohesion and meaning long-term. A community that abandons its
  vulnerable will collapse from the inside.
\item
  \textbf{Vetting newcomers.} Everyone gets a trial period. Observe
  behavior under stress. Do they share? Do they lie? Do they pull their
  weight? Three strikes is generous; two is practical.
\end{enumerate}

Channels}\label{group-communication-channels}

As your group grows, communication complexity explodes. The number of
pairwise communication channels in a group of \(n\) people is:

\[C(n) = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}\]

This is why small groups feel ``tight'' and large groups feel
``bureaucratic'' --- the communication channels scale quadratically. You

  ``communication overhead'' problem. For \(n > 10\), most organizations
  introduce hierarchical or network structures to reduce the effective
  number of channels any one person must maintain. See: March, J.G. \&
  Simon, H.A. \emph{Organizations} (1958).}

\subsubsection{Dunbar Numbers --- The Social Brain
Hypothesis}\label{dunbar-numbers-the-social-brain-hypothesis}

  apply to face-to-face relationships; digital communication may extend
  some layers but doesn't fundamentally change the cognitive

\subsubsection{Self-Defense (Last
Resort)}\label{self-defense-last-resort}

The math of violence is always bad: winner is still injured and spent
resources; loser is dead or vengeful; avoider is intact with resources
preserved.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Situational awareness.} See threats before they become fights.
  The best fight is the one that never happens.
\item
  \textbf{Barriers.} Doors, walls, fences, distance. Obstacles buy time,
  and time buys options.
\item
  \textbf{Improvised weapons.} Reach (sticks, pipes) \textgreater{} edge
  (knives, glass) \textgreater{} weight (rocks, hammers). Reach keeps
  them away. That's the whole point.
\item
  \textbf{Numbers.} A group of 3 is harder to attack than a group of 1.
  This is the fundamental math of defense.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Water · Energy · Hygiene at
Scale}\label{water-energy-hygiene-at-scale}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Water:} 3 liters per person per day (drinking + cooking). 10
  people = 30L/day. A well producing 100L/day supports \textasciitilde30
  people.
\item
  \textbf{Energy:} One solar panel (\textasciitilde300W) supports basic
  lighting + phone charging for a small group. Heating and cooking
  require significantly more. Prioritize: cooking \textgreater{} light
  \textgreater{} comfort.
\item
  \textbf{Hygiene:} Latrines must scale with population. 1 latrine per
  20 people, minimum. A cholera outbreak in a group of 50 with no
  sanitation will kill more than any zombie ever did.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Ostrom's 8 Principles for Common-Pool Resource
Governance}\label{ostroms-8-principles-for-common-pool-resource-governance}

Elinor Ostrom won the Nobel Prize in Economics (2009) for her work on
how communities govern shared resources without top-down control. Her 8
design principles are the closest thing to a proven blueprint for
self-governance:\footnote{Ostrom, E. \emph{Governing the Commons: The
  Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action} (1990). Cambridge
  University Press. Ostrom's 8 principles were derived from
  meta-analysis of long-enduring common-pool resource institutions
  worldwide. She demonstrated that communities can self-govern without
  either privatization or state control --- a finding that overturned
  decades of conventional wisdom (the ``tragedy of the commons''
  narrative). These principles are directly applicable to any
  post-crisis community managing shared resources.}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Clearly defined boundaries} --- Who can use the resource? Who
  can't?
\item
  \textbf{Congruence with local conditions} --- Rules fit the actual
  situation, not an abstract ideal
\item
  \textbf{Participatory decision-making} --- People affected by the
  rules help make them
\item
  \textbf{Monitoring} --- Someone watches the resource and the
  rule-followers
\item
  \textbf{Graduated sanctions} --- Small violations get small penalties,
  not exile
\item
  \textbf{Conflict resolution mechanisms} --- Fast, cheap, fair dispute
  resolution
\item
  \textbf{Minimal recognition of rights} --- External authorities don't
  undermine local governance
\item
  \textbf{Nested enterprises} --- Large systems are built from smaller,
  self-governing units
\end{enumerate}

These principles emerged from studying fisheries, irrigation systems,
forests, and pastures --- real communities managing real scarce
resources over centuries. They work because they align incentives with
consequences. A community that follows all 8 is remarkably resilient. A
community that ignores them is, historically, short-lived.

\subsubsection{Psychology of the Masses / Sociology
Basics}\label{psychology-of-the-masses-sociology-basics}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Us vs.~Them.} The most powerful social force. It can unite
  against an external threat --- or split from within. If someone is
  trying to convince you that a subgroup within your community is the
  enemy, \emph{that person is the actual threat}.
\item
  \textbf{Conformity pressure.} Solomon Asch showed people will deny the
  evidence of their own eyes to agree with a group. If everyone says the
  water is safe and you think it isn't --- speak up. Dissent is a
  survival skill.
\item
  \textbf{Diffusion of responsibility.} ``Someone else will handle it.''
  Assign specific tasks to specific people. ``Someone check the
  perimeter'' = nobody checks. ``Anna, check the perimeter at 2100'' =
  perimeter gets checked.
\item
  \textbf{Authority capture.} In crisis, people defer to whoever seems
  most confident. Confidence ≠ competence. The quiet person with the
  notebook is probably right.
\end{itemize}

Pyramid}\label{iasc-intervention-pyramid}

The Inter-Agency Standing Committee (IASC) defines a pyramid of mental

  Emergency Settings} (2007). The 4-layer pyramid is the global standard

  this guide follows by prioritizing physical safety before
  psychological support.}

\begin{verbatim}
                    ╱╲
                   ╱    ╲
                  ╱  4.  ╲     Specialized services
                 ╱  Clinical ╲   (psychiatry, specialized therapy)
                ╱──────────────╲
               ╱    3. Focused  ╲   Non-specialized support
              ╱   non-specialized ╲  (trained counselors, support groups)
             ╱──────────────────────╲
            ╱   2. Community & family  ╲   Social support
           ╱     support mechanisms      ╲  (group activities, peer support)
          ╱────────────────────────────────╲
         ╱   1. Basic services & security    ╲   Foundational needs

  or do you want suggestions?'' Respect the answer.

  \textbf{Don't minimize.} Avoid ``it could be worse'' or ``just think
  positive.'' Well-intentioned and deeply unhelpful.
\item
  \textbf{Stay connected.} Check in regularly, even with a simple
  ``thinking of you.'' Isolation amplifies everything.
\item
  \textbf{Know your limits.} You are a friend, not a therapist. If the
  situation exceeds your capacity, say so with love: ``I care about you
  and I think you need more support than I can give. Can I help you find
  someone?''
\item
  \textbf{Take care of yourself.} Empathy is finite. Refill your own
  reserves → 📍 Calm Guide (Ch.4).
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Self-Defense Law (Germany --- §32 StGB
Notwehr)}\label{self-defense-law-germany-32-stgb-notwehr}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  You may defend yourself against a \textbf{present, unlawful attack}
  against yourself or another person.\footnote{Strafgesetzbuch (StGB)
    §32 Notwehr: ``Wer eine Tat begeht, die durch Notwehr geboten ist,
    handelt nicht rechtswidrig.'' §33 extends leniency for excessive
    self-defense committed under ``confusion, fear, or terror''
    (Verwirrung, Furcht oder Schrecken).}
\item
  The defense must be \textbf{necessary} --- no less harmful alternative
  available.
\item
  The defense must be \textbf{proportionate} --- you can't use deadly
  force to protect property alone.
\item
  No duty to retreat in Germany, but retreating is always the safer
  legal strategy.
\item
  If you acted in self-defense and are unsure about the legal outcome →
  contact a lawyer \textbf{before} making any statements to police.
\end{itemize}
